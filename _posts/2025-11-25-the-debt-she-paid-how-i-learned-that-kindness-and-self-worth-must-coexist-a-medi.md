‚ÄãThere is a truth about human nature no one teaches you directly. You learn it through proximity, through watching, until the conclusion becomes undeniable.
‚ÄãIt is this:** People seek advantage.** It is how they survive and thrive. It is the vantage point from which they view every relationship, every outstretched hand. And if you let them, they will take whatever advantage you offer.

‚ÄãShe was a woman who loved to talk. To give. To share whatever she had, even when what she had was barely enough for herself. Her door was never closed. Her time was never fully her own. ‚ÄãAnd I watched many of those people take advantage of her. Slowly, persistently, in the quiet way that exploitation often works‚Äîthrough assumption, through entitlement, through the gradual understanding that this woman would give and give and never demand anything in return.

‚ÄãShe had a friend, a woman from the United States, who saw my mother‚Äôs situation: capable, hardworking, but limited by geography and circumstance. The friend had an idea: a work-and-travel program called

‚ÄãMy mother received this news the way parched earth receives rain. She was full of possibility, full of the belief that finally, after years of giving to others, something was coming back to her.

‚ÄãThen came the message.
‚ÄãThe friend realized something: My mother was "better off" than many others. There were people who needed this opportunity more. People who deserved it more.
‚ÄãThe program would go forward. But my mother would not be part of it.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*iypun36DW0kLfvLLIgQ8LA.jpeg)
‚ÄãHere is what stayed with me about that moment:
‚ÄãMy mother tried to be a good host. And the very evidence of her effort, the welcoming home, the full table, the generosity on display‚Äîbecame the reason she was deemed undeserving.
‚ÄãShe tried to show up well, and her hospitality was used to measure her as "better off."
‚ÄãI have never been able to accept that logic. The idea that showing up well disqualifies you from receiving. The idea that your willingness to sacrifice is proof that you do not need help. It is a convenient conclusion for people who want to redirect their generosity elsewhere.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*2506DTRFVwwtPFrTGkeOEQ.jpeg)
**‚Äã**The irony is profound: Cape Cod Workers launched and changed thousands of lives. Among those thousands were my mother‚Äôs own sisters, brothers, and relatives. The program that had passed her by became a bridge that carried the people she loved to better circumstances.
‚ÄãMy mother was genuinely happy about this.

‚ÄãShe had three children, and at different points, raised every single one of her siblings' children, too. Her resources were never hers alone.
‚ÄãYet, she was "better off." And so the opportunity went elsewhere.
‚Äã*The people who give the most are often the ones deemed to need the least. Because they make it look easy. Because their generosity is so consistent that it becomes invisible, like air.*
‚ÄãThis is the trap that kindness can become if you are not careful.
‚Äã**The Inheritance I am Choosing to Carry Forward**

‚ÄãI did not fully understand my mother‚Äôs kindness while she was alive. I wondered if it was wisdom or weakness.
‚ÄãIt was only after she died that I saw it clearly. Her kindness was not naivety. She knew what people were. She understood the transaction she was making every time she gave without expectation of return. She gave anyway.
‚ÄãShe had decided she would rather be herself than be protected. I understand the courage it takes to remain soft in a world that rewards hardness.
‚ÄãBut understanding is not the same as imitation.
‚ÄãI see my mother in me now. The same instinct to help. But I have also learned the lesson she perhaps knew but did not act on:
‚ÄãWhen you are kind, not everyone repays that kindness in return. Some people feel grateful. Others feel entitled. And a certain kind of person sees only opportunity‚Äîa resource to be extracted, a well to be drained.
‚ÄãThis is not a reason to stop being kind. It is a reason to be kind with open eyes.
‚ÄãI have watched people acknowledge my talent and yet compensate me as though my value were negotiable. I have watched people I helped turn around and help others first, because they had categorized me as someone who would wait.
‚ÄãThis is what happens when you teach people that your kindness has no limits. They learn the lesson. They act accordingly.
‚Äã**Kindness Is Not An Infinite Resource**

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*Q1Hfaw747iyTMwOXgQa-1g.jpeg)
‚ÄãMy mother‚Äôs way was beautiful. It was also costly. She gave the same way to everyone‚Äîthe people who would honor her generosity and the people who would exploit it received the same open heart.
‚ÄãI am learning to do it differently. Not to stop giving, but to watch. To notice who reciprocates and who only receives. To pay attention to the patterns before I invest myself fully.
‚ÄãYour kindness is valuable. It is one of the most valuable things you have. And valuable things deserve to be protected‚Äînot hoarded, but placed intentionally, given to people who have shown they will treat it with care.
‚ÄãThis is not cynicism. This is wisdom.
‚ÄãI am trying to honor the beauty of her generosity without repeating the cost of her missing boundaries. I am grateful for her kindness. And I will honor it by becoming something that builds on who she was:
‚ÄãA person who gives generously and values themselves fully. A person who loves with open hands but knows what those hands are worth.
‚Äã**The Final Lesson She Never Said Aloud**

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*9cypB_RfOd8YeKcV4RjIHQ.jpeg)
‚ÄãFor my mother, who gave everything and complained about it and gave some more. And for anyone learning that kindness and self-worth can live in the same heart:
‚ÄãBe kind. But know your worth.
